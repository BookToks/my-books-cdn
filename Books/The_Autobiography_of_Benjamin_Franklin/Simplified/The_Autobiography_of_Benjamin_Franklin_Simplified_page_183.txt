He saw his academy as a "foundation for future generations to build a bigger and better school that fits their needs." To make this happen, the rules were written and signed on November 13, 1749. Twenty-four respected people from Philadelphia were chosen as trustees. Franklin worked mostly with Thomas Hopkinson, the Rev. Richard Peters, who was the province's secretary, Tench Francis, the attorney general, and Dr. Phineas Bond to make these plans.

The next rule shows a kind and helpful attitude that we should all try to copy. We hope this rule is still followed today to bring honor to our city.

It says that if a teacher or principal who gets paid from the school's funds becomes sick or can't work and falls into poverty, the trustees can help support them based on how much they need and deserve, using the school's money.
