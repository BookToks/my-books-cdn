It was first translated into Persian, and from there it reached Europe. Later, Raja Ram Mohun Roy made an English version. Since then, it has been translated into many languages. Writers in English, German, and French all agree that it is one of the best explanations of the religion and ideas in the Vedas.

Sir Edwin Arnold made it popular by turning it into a poem called "The Secret of Death." Ralph Waldo Emerson also briefly told the story at the end of his essay about "Immortality." People do not agree on where this Upanishad fits in Vedic literature. Some say it belongs to the Yajur-Veda, others to the Sama-Veda, and many think it is part of the Atharva-Veda. The story first appears in the Rig-Veda, is told more clearly in the Yajur-Veda, and is fully explained in the Katha-Upanishad with the highest Vedic teachings. However, there is no clear proof about the exact place of this final version, and the meaning of the name Katha is still unknown.

The text is a conversation between a young student named Nachiketas and the Ruler of Death about what happens after death.
