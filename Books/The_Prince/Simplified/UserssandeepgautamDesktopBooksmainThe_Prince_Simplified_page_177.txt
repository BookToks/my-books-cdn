Maximinus Gaius Julius Verus Maximinus (around 173–238) was a Roman emperor and a soldier born in Thrace from a poor background. He became a strong military leader and led an army rebellion against the young emperor Alexander Severus, who was abandoned by his own troops and murdered. The Senate disliked Maximinus, and he faced many rebellions, which he crushed harshly until his own soldiers killed him.

Paulo Orsini was the leader of the Orsini family during Cesare Borgia’s rise to power. He accepted an invitation to negotiate at Senigallia but was strangled when he arrived.

Moses was a Hebrew leader from the Old Testament who led the Jews out of slavery in Egypt to the "promised land."

Nabis ruled Sparta from 207 to 192 BC and was determined to make Sparta powerful again. After expanding its territory, he was attacked by the Romans and their allies. He was defeated but held on to Sparta for a while before being killed by his enemies.

Pertinax was a Roman emperor for three months in 193. He became emperor after the assassination of Commodus but was killed by soldiers because he couldn’t give them the money they wanted and made strict rules that bothered them.

Petrarch (1304–74) was a scholar, poet, and early Humanist. His work on sonnets influenced European poetry for centuries. Some lines in Machiavelli’s The Prince are from Petrarch’s poems, where he asks Italian leaders to stop using foreign soldiers in their wars.

Oliverotto Euffreducci (around 1475–1502) was a mercenary leader who took power in Fermo in 1502 and used force to kill his enemies. He was later killed by Borgia at Senigallia.

Pandolfo Petrucci (1452–1512) was a powerful leader in Siena from 1487. His group overthrew their enemies in a coup, and he became the ruler while keeping the town’s official government. Machiavelli talked with him several times as Florence’s ambassador.

Remirro de Orco (around 1452–1502) was a military leader who worked for Cesare Borgia. He fought in many battles and was made governor of Romagna in 1501. He was arrested for corruption and executed in 1502.

Machiavelli mentions two Philips of Macedonia. The first, Philip II (382–336 BC), was the father of Alexander the Great. He came to power after his older brothers died and rebuilt Macedonia through wars and smart deals. He was killed by one of his bodyguards.

The second was Philip V (238–178 BC), who followed a successful policy of expanding Macedonia until the Romans defeated him after beating the Carthaginians in 200 BC.
