Philopoemen (253–183 BC) was a Greek leader and general who fought many battles with the Achaean army.

Pyrrhus (318–272 BC) was the King of Epirus and a very successful military commander. He was a big threat to the Romans in southern Italy and Sicily and also fought the Carthaginians. His costly win at the Battle of Asculum in 279 BC led to the term ‘Pyrrhic victory,’ meaning a win that costs too much.

Giorgio Scali was one of the leaders of the Ciompi (wool-workers) rebellion in Florence in 1378. He tried to stop the rulers from punishing a friend but was arrested and beheaded in 1382.

Romulus is the legendary founder and first king of Rome.

Scipio Africanus (c. 234–183 BC) was a Roman general famous for defeating Hannibal at the Battle of Zama in 201 BC. This big win earned him the name Africanus. After being accused of taking bribes, he left Rome and lived by the coast of Campania.

Georges d’Amboise (1460–1510) was a cardinal and later Archbishop of Rouen. He was an adviser to the Duke of Orleans, who became King Louis XII of France. Louis made d’Amboise his prime minister and convinced Alexander VI to make him a cardinal to improve relations between France and the Pope. D’Amboise supported Louis’s campaigns in Italy and worked with Cesare Borgia to try to make himself pope after Alexander VI died.

Lucius Septimius Severus (146–211) was a Roman emperor known for making the Roman government more military-focused. After some emperors were murdered, Severus took control with his army in 193. He defeated rivals who tried to claim the throne and spent his last years fighting in Britain. He died in York in 211.

Giuliano de’ Rovere became Pope Julius II.

Cardinal Raffaello Riario of Savona was a powerful church leader.

Roberto da San Severino was a mercenary commander who led Venetian forces in 1482 and died fighting for Venice in 1487.

Saul was the first king of Israel, chosen by the people around 1025 BC.

Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498) was born in Ferrara. He studied philosophy and medicine before becoming a Dominican friar. He started preaching in Florence from 1482 to 1487 but was mostly ignored. Then Lorenzo de’ Medici called him back to lead the monastery of San Marco in 1490. Savonarola gave sermons against corruption and warned of doom and foreign invasion. When Charles VIII invaded Italy in 1494 and the Medici family fled, many people believed Savonarola was right. He took charge of Florence as a religious leader from 1494 to 1498 and encouraged people to burn anything sinful like books and paintings in what became known as the Bonfire of the Vanities.

Ascanio Sforza (1455–1505) was the fifth child of Francesco Sforza, Duke of Milan. He was made a cardinal in 1484 and tried several times to become pope but failed. He worked as a spy for Milan in Rome but was kicked out when Milan helped the French invade Italy in 1494. Later, he was brought back but lost power when the French took over Milan under Louis XII. He was imprisoned by the French for three years before Georges d’Amboise helped get him released.
