1910 He takes a job in New York as the Director of Publications and Research for the NAACP, a group fighting for civil rights. He starts and edits their monthly magazine called The Crisis.

1911 He goes to a meeting in London called the Universal Races Congress. He publishes his first novel, The Quest of the Silver Fleece. He also joins the Socialist Party.

1912 He supports Woodrow Wilson to become president and leaves the Socialist Party.

1913 He writes and puts on a show called The Star of Ethiopia to celebrate 50 years since the Emancipation Proclamation.

1914 He writes articles in The Crisis supporting women’s right to vote. He says World War I started because European countries were fighting over colonies, especially in Africa, but he still supports the Allies in the war.

1915 Booker T. Washington dies in November. He publishes a book called The Negro. He leads protests against the movie The Birth of a Nation because it spreads false and harmful ideas.

1918 In July, he writes an article called ‘Close Ranks’ in The Crisis, telling black people to support the war. In December, he travels to Europe to check on black soldiers for the NAACP.

1919 He organizes the First Pan-African Congress in Paris, where 57 people from the US, the Caribbean, Europe, and Africa come together.

1920 He publishes Darkwater: Voices from within the Veil, a book with essays, stories, and poems. He starts and edits The Brownies’ Book, a magazine for black kids.

1921 The Second Pan-African Congress is held in London, Brussels, and Paris.

1922 He pushes for the Dyer Anti-Lynching Bill to stop lynching, but it gets blocked in the US Senate.

1923 The Third Pan-African Congress happens in Lisbon.

1924 He publishes The Gift of Black Folk: The Negroes in the Making of America.

1925 His essay ‘The Negro Mind Reaches Out’ first appears in Foreign Affairs and is included in The New Negro, an important book of Harlem Renaissance writings edited by Alain Locke.
